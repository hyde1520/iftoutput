Risk Assessment

The process of wildfire risk assessment is concerned with changes in expected loss to values at risk in response to fuel treatments, structure improvements, and assumptions about fire weather and suppression capabilities. The risk assessment work flow provides tools to use to:

  • Examine values at risk and the relative risk of fire occurring on the landscape to help identify areas that may warrant fuels treatment.
  • Develop values-at-risk maps.
  • Model fire behavior and the probability of ignition across the landscape.
  • Model the expected loss or benefit for values at risk resulting from fire at given flame lengths.
  • Download and edit LANDFIRE landscape (.lcp ) data.

There are two approaches to performing a risk assessment; they both use the same steps, though there are differences in the math performed. The steps are as follows:

  1. Set up a project in IFTDSS and acquire LANDFIRE data.
  2. Create a run using one of the two risk pathways.
    • Worst-case flame lengths approach.
    • Flame length probabilities approach.
  3. Develop a "values at risk" map.
    • Assign each value at risk a response function.
  4. Provide environmental and simulation input values to the modules.
    • FlamMap fire behavior.
    • RANDIG burn probability.
    • Flame length classes.
  5. Review and edit the spatial landscape data.
  6. Analyze potential fire behavior and burn probability output data.
  7. Identify potential fire risks across a landscape.
    • Review the default relative net value change data (for more information on this step, see the risk assessment tutorials).
  8. Classify the net value change data.
    • This functionality makes it possible for you to narrow your search of high fire hazard and risk areas to areas with the greatest potential loss.
  9. Review the map summary data.
    • Compare your relative net value change distribution to the default relative net value change distribution.
  10. Export the map summary data.
    • Export the map summary data to review the data using Google Earth.
